# wedin-live-act-intelligence
## MIL Skill Update — Live Act Intelligence Framework
## Discovery Session Output — April 2026

This document replaces the ensemble-to-room-size section in wedin-mil-SKILL.md and adds the complete live act intelligence framework. It is the output of a structured discovery session with Rus Nerwich (founder, 100+ weddings at Tones of Note). Every rule here is grounded in real-world experience, not theory.

Load this alongside CLAUDE.md and wedin-mil-SKILL.md for any session touching MIL logic, deep-dive questions, or brief generation.

---

## Why This Framework Exists

The most common and most expensive mistake couples make is booking a live act based on how it sounds in concept, without understanding whether it will actually work in the room. The MIL's job is to close that gap — to translate what a couple imagines into what will actually happen on the day, and to tell them honestly when those two things don't match.

This framework gives the MIL the specialist knowledge to do that. It is not a list of rules for the sake of rules. Every principle below comes from watching these mistakes happen at real weddings and understanding why they happen.

---

## The Four Core Mistakes wedin.ai Prevents

Every MIL recommendation must be evaluated against all four before it is surfaced.

**Mistake 1 — Novelty over function**
Couples book acts because they sound exciting in concept without understanding whether the ensemble, its size, instrumentation, amplification, and style will actually hold the room. The test is not "does this sound good in my head?" — it is "will this create the vibe and carry the space the couple is imagining?" If the physics don't support the concept, the MIL must say so and redirect.

**Mistake 2 — Incorrect instrumentation**
There is clear logic governing which instruments work together and which don't. Instrumentation must be evaluated against the moment, the room size, the style and genre, and the song choices. Never recommend an ensemble configuration that violates instrumentation logic.

**Mistake 3 — Classical music without knowledge**
Classical acts have stylistic and repertoire limitations couples don't anticipate. Never recommend a classical act without flagging what to confirm before booking.

**Mistake 4 — Assuming the band plays the music you want**
Couples assume. Acts have narrower ranges than they appear to. Every live act recommendation must surface the five vetting questions.

## The Function vs Feature Rule

**This is the most important structural rule in the framework.**

Any unamplified or roaming act must be positioned as a feature with a defined duration — never as the primary room carrier for a moment.

A roaming guitar and violinist sounds magical in concept. Above 40 people without amplification, only the guests at nearby tables can hear it. For the rest of the room, the music is non-existent. The act fails its function entirely.

The mariachi band at dinner is different: it is booked as a feature for a defined period, a cultural novelty, not as the room's primary music carrier. This distinction is the difference between a booking that works and one that disappoints.

**Rule:** When a couple's answers suggest an unamplified or roaming act as a primary moment carrier, the MIL must redirect. Surface it as a feature option with a defined duration where appropriate. Never as the primary recommendation.

---

## The Live vs PA Golden Rule

Before recommending live for any moment, evaluate: does this song, played live with this instrumentation, do the moment justice?

**Never recommend live for live's sake.** If a song doesn't translate well to the available ensemble — whether because of the arrangement, the instrumentation, or the style — the PA is the right answer. A great recording played well is better than a mediocre live version of the wrong song.

This rule applies most critically to:
- First dance (if choreographed to a specific recording — PA only, always)
- Ceremony songs with specific emotional weight
- Any moment where the couple has named specific songs

**Songs are the ground truth.** When songs are provided, the MIL evaluates each one against the recommended ensemble before confirming the recommendation. The song determines the format — the format does not determine the song.

## The Arc Principle

Don't give everything away at once. Restraint and sequencing are part of the product intelligence.

A PA-played curated playlist for arrivals, with live musicians entering for ceremony, creates a deliberate perceptible shift — the ceremony feels more significant. This is a considered creative choice, not a budget compromise. The MIL should offer this framing explicitly.

The same logic applies across the day: background music during pre-drinks, live band beginning at dinner, gives the live music arrival the feeling of an event.

Silence between moments is also a legitimate production tool. Guests moving between spaces with a brief moment of silence, then music beginning once everyone is seated, makes the band's entrance more impactful. The MIL surfaces this as a creative option — never frames it as a gap.

## The Solo Musician Ceiling Rule

A solo musician — regardless of how they're presented — has a weight and energy ceiling. The ceiling is not just about guest count. It is about the function the solo musician is being asked to perform.

**Solo works for:**
- Arrivals (any size, with appropriate amplification outdoors)
- Ceremony (any size — concentrated attention, quiet room)
- Intimate pre-drinks (up to approximately 60 guests in a small room)
- Dinner up to 60 guests in a small room

**Solo never works as the primary carrier for:**
- Dinner with 80+ guests
- Pre-drinks with 80+ guests unless backed by a track or looping pedal
- Any dancing moment

**The grand piano exception:** A grand piano centred in a room as a visual production feature is a different proposition to a keyboard player on a stage built for a band. The former is a production decision and can extend the solo option into larger spaces. The latter looks wrong and sounds wrong. The MIL distinguishes between these two situations when a couple mentions solo piano for a large dinner.

**Redirect logic:** If a couple wants solo piano for a large dinner, the MIL offers three honest alternatives: a centrepiece grand as a production feature (requires a specific venue), a trio playing that style, or a curated DJ playlist in that style.

## Ensemble Instrumentation Logic

Ensemble size alone is not the right frame. Instrumentation must follow harmonic and rhythmic logic. The MIL reasons about configuration, not just headcount.

**Solo (1 musician)**
Piano or guitar only. One chordal/harmonic instrument.

**Duo — three valid configurations**

*Configuration 2a — Chordal + melody line:*
Piano or guitar + vocals, or piano/guitar + horn (sax, trumpet), or piano/guitar + violin. The most versatile duo format. Works across arrivals, ceremony, pre-drinks, dinner. The melody instrument must be chosen based on the song repertoire and whether the couple wants vocals or instrumental — not chosen in isolation.

*Configuration 2b — Chordal + upright bass:*
Jazz or light instrumental only. Particularly strong if the chordal player sings. Works for pre-drinks and dinner. Intimate, sophisticated feel.

*Configuration 2c — Guitar + percussion:*
Bossa nova and Latin-adjacent styles. Natural foundation for a trio — add bass. Works for pre-drinks and dinner where the couple wants rhythmic warmth without a full band.

**Trio (3 musicians)**
Any duo configuration + upright bass preferred. Acoustic, folk, or jazz. Minimum recommended for dinner with 60+ guests. Holds a room with genuine presence. Works for pre-drinks at most guest counts.

**Quartet (4 musicians)**
Trio + drummer/percussionist. Full rhythmic foundation. Appropriate for dinner and pre-drinks at larger weddings. Can carry dancing for a defined period before DJ takeover.

**Quintet — minimum for dancing (5 musicians)**
Quartet + horn (trumpet or sax). Keys or guitar, bass, drums, vocals, horn. This is a full function band. Primary recommendation for dancing at Band 3–5. Has the weight and energy to carry a large room for 70–90 minutes. Anything under 5 piece for dancing cannot sustain the variety, intensity, and energy required. This is a firm rule.

## Amplification Rules

**Outdoors:** Any act playing outdoors to more than 40 guests must be amplified. No exceptions. Flag this in every outdoor recommendation.

**Ceremony:** PA infrastructure almost always present for the officiant. Keyboard and bass players bring their own amplification. Horns and drums rarely amplified at ceremony. Strings rarely amplified unless very large ensemble or very large guest count. Vocals need amplification outdoors; inside a church with good acoustics, not necessary.

**Church:** The resident organist changes the ensemble recommendation entirely for ceremony and arrivals. Always flag this for couples with a church ceremony.

## DJ PLUS — Removed as Standard Recommendation

DJ PLUS is no longer a default mid-budget recommendation. It is removed from the MIL's standard architecture.

The principle: when a producer makes a song, it was complete as released. Adding a live musician on top of a DJ set generally works against the music rather than with it. The exception is a very specific, limited feature moment with full coordination — and only when it genuinely serves the music rather than performing novelty.

DJ PLUS may appear as a bold direction option in rare circumstances only. Never as the default recommendation for any budget tier.

---

## The 70–90 Minute Live Dance Band Rule

One long set for the live dance band, then DJ takeover. The DJ takeover is a strategic programming decision, not a downgrade.

A skilled DJ reads the band's last song and mixes in something appropriate — same tempo and feel for a seamless continuation, or a deliberate gear change that shifts the mood and energy. The transition must be briefed specifically.

Exception: very intimate weddings in small venues where the band can move fluidly between dance hits and background music and the intimacy of the space keeps guests together regardless.

## Room-Holding Variables — Evaluation Order

When assessing whether an act can hold a room, evaluate in this order:

1. Guest count
2. Indoor vs outdoor
3. Venue size and acoustic character
4. Amplification
5. Ensemble size
6. Instrumentation configuration
7. Style and genre
8. The specific wedding moment and its function

---

## The Five Vetting Questions

Every live act recommendation must surface these questions. These are the things couples don't know to ask — and the things that determine whether the booking actually delivers.

1. Ask for a set list — what songs do they actually play?
2. Are they open to couple-requested songs if provided in advance?
3. Do they use a backing track? This affects the sound and feel significantly — it should be a known choice, not a surprise.
4. What styles and genres can they genuinely play — not just cover on request?
5. Is this a fixed lineup or do they use deps? Deputy musicians can change the quality of the act.

**Additional for dancing sets:**
6. Do songs run into each other continuously or are there breaks between them? Breaks create dead air on the dance floor.

## Moment-by-Moment Framework

### Guest Arrivals

**Function:** Tone-setting, state-shifting, anticipation-building. Moves guests from ordinary day to something different. The specific vibe is couple-dependent and known from the discovery session and moment answers.

**The arc principle applies here first:** PA-played curated playlist for arrivals with live musicians entering for ceremony creates a deliberate shift — the ceremony feels more significant. This is a considered choice, not a budget compromise. Surface it as an option, always.

**Ensemble logic:**
- The arrivals ensemble and ceremony ensemble should be the same musicians. Different repertoire or configuration is fine — two completely separate ensembles is wrong and poor value.
- Solo through 4-piece works. Don't be overly prescriptive — a solo violinist during seating is some couples' dream. Respect it while flagging the amplification requirement above 40 people outdoors.
- String ensembles (solo through quartet) are the classical arrivals standard. May add keyboard depending on repertoire.
- Church weddings often default to the resident organist — flag this, it changes the recommendation.

**Duration rule:** Arrivals typically 45 minutes. Musicians play 30–40 minutes maximum. Don't brief or book for longer.

**Amplification rule:** Outdoors, more than 40 guests — amplification required. No exceptions.

**Critical mistake:** Unamplified act outdoors at scale. Wrong style and repertoire for the moment — the job is building toward something, the music must serve that arc.

**High-production exception:** Full orchestra, big band, or flash mob for arrivals leading into ceremony. These exist and are spectacular. They require a completely different level of creative curation and coordination — flag as exceptional production requiring specialist planning, never a standard recommendation.

**Budget framing:** Budget for 1 hour of musician time. The same musicians will likely cover ceremony. One booking, two moments — efficient use of spend.

---

### Ceremony

**Function:** Heighten emotional intensity, punctuate key sub-moments, hold the energy and space. Creates a cohesive soundtrack from the processional to the couple leaving married. This is the emotional peak of the day.

**Sub-moment architecture:** Four distinct music cues — groom/groomsmen processional, bride's processional, signing of the register, recessional. Each can be treated differently. A skilled approach uses the ensemble creatively — stripped configuration for the groom's entrance, building instrumentation as the bride approaches, hitting the emotional peak at her arrival.

**The live vs PA golden rule applies most critically here:** Every song must be evaluated on whether it translates well when played live with the available instrumentation. Never live for live's sake. If the ensemble cannot do the song justice, PA is the right answer.

**Ensemble configurations:**
- Solo guitar or piano is most common.
- Melody instrument added based on song choice and whether the couple wants vocals or instrumental. The melody instrument is chosen based on the repertoire, not in isolation.
- Trios play ceremony but are less common.
- String and classical ensembles work at any size from solo to quartet.
- Full jazz band at ceremony is rare and generally wrong for the moment.

**Amplification at ceremony:**
- PA infrastructure almost always present for the officiant.
- Keyboard and bass players bring their own amplification.
- Horns and drums rarely amplified at ceremony.
- Strings rarely amplified unless very large ensemble or very large guest count.
- Vocals need amplification outdoors; inside a church with good acoustics, not necessary.

**Timing and cues — the primary failure mode:** Musicians not plugged into the ceremony order and the couple's arrival timing. Results in a choppy, stop-start experience that breaks the emotional flow entirely. The act must be briefed on exact cues, who signals them, and what happens if timing shifts. Band too loud is the second most common failure — ceremony requires restraint.

**Coordinator brief requirement:** Name the cue person for each processional. Name each music cue. Specify who signals the act, when, and what the contingency is if timing shifts.

---

### Pre-Drinks

**Function:** Set a great vibe, facilitate socialising, keep energy alive while guests mingle. The music holds the room in the couple's absence — it is the host when there is no host.

**The one act rule:** Never more than one act during pre-drinks. Either DJ, PA background music, or one live act. Not two.

**Ensemble configurations by style:**

*Pop, soul, contemporary:* Up to 80 guests, solo guitar or piano works but almost always with a backing track or looping pedal to add weight and substance. Solo without a backing track or loop risks having no gravitas in the room. The musician will typically sing as well.

*Jazz:* Guitar with sax or trumpet is the most effective duo. The guitarist walks bass lines and comps underneath the horn's melody — harmonic and rhythmic completeness as a two-piece. Piano and sax works but has less rhythmic feel. From there, follows the ensemble instrumentation framework.

*Reggae, African, contemporary:* Trio to 5-piece is appropriate and common.

*Marimba:* Popular in SA but must be chosen carefully. The sound is novel and can feel overbearing if sustained for 60 minutes. Flag explicitly: marimba works as a feature for a defined period, not as continuous pre-drinks music.

**Duration and set management:** Pre-drinks typically 1 hour. Band plays the full hour if running on time. If running late, coordinator splits the set into two with background music in between — style consistent with the couple's taste profile from their session answers. Coordinator must communicate timing changes to the band proactively.

**The arc principle applies:** PA background music during pre-drinks, live band beginning at dinner, creates a deliberate arc. Surface this as a considered option.

**Silence as a tool:** Transitions between pre-drinks and dinner don't need to be filled. Guests moving to the reception venue with a brief moment of silence, then music beginning once seated, makes the band's entrance more impactful. Frame as a creative choice, never a gap.

**Two failure modes:**
Too much — DJ or band playing full-on dance music at high volume. Pre-drinks is about talking and connecting. Music that dominates conversation kills the function of the moment.
Too little — a live act with no gravitas, too small, unamplified, unable to create any vibe. In this case a great curated playlist would have served better. The honest recommendation when budget or logistics don't support a credible live act: don't do a live act.

---

### Your Entrance

**Function:** Pure celebration. The couple's first appearance as married. One moment, one job — make it feel like an arrival.

**Almost always PA, not live:** The entrance is typically 30–60 seconds. A live band for 30–60 seconds makes no logistical sense unless the band is going straight into a full set immediately after — which is unusual. Default recommendation is DJ or PA.

**Song selection:** Instantly recognisable, celebratory, something the couple genuinely loves. Must land emotionally for the whole room — not just the couple.

**After the entrance:** Best man speech, MC welcome, house rules typically follow immediately. Music fades to background. DJ reads the room and transitions. This transition must be briefed explicitly.

**The cuing rule:** Coordinator tells the DJ exactly when to start. If no coordinator, this responsibility must be explicitly assigned — best man or MC. Ambiguity here directly causes the two failure modes.

**Coordinator brief requirement:** Name the cue person. Name the song. Name what comes after the song and who manages that transition.

**Failure modes:** Wrong song cued. Timing off due to poor communication. Both entirely preventable with a named cue person and a clear run sheet.

---

### Dinner

**Function:** Facilitate conversation, hold the room together, create atmosphere. Punctuated by speeches. Music works in the background but everyone would notice if it stopped or went wrong.

**The dance band principle:** If there is a dance band booked for dancing, they should almost always play dinner too. A band that only plays 60–90 minutes of dancing is unusual and poor value. Same ensemble covers dinner and dancing — this is the standard architecture.

**Ensemble minimum for live dinner music:** Trio and up, always. Whether instrumental or with vocals. A duo can work in specific configurations but trio is the honest minimum for a room of seated guests.

**Repertoire logic for dinner:** Jazz standards, bossa nova, recognisable classics — music that is easy to digest, widely loved, and doesn't demand active listening. In the SA context, Afro-pop works. The test: does it translate well live with the available ensemble? If not, PA.

**Live vs PA decision tree:**
- Dance band present → play dinner live unless repertoire is incompatible with the ensemble.
- No dance band → trio to quartet for dinner if budget supports it.
- Budget doesn't support a credible trio → great curated playlist over PA is the honest recommendation.
- Couple insists on a style that doesn't translate live → PA for dinner, live for dancing.

**Speeches and music:** Band takes a break during speeches. Sound engineer or DJ handles the speeches period. Band does not stop and start around individual speeches. Coordinator must share the speech schedule with the band and sound engineer in advance.

**Volume — the most common dinner failure:** Band must understand that dinner volume is a service to conversation. Coordinator must be empowered — and given explicit language — to ask the band to lower volume. This is not optional.

**Coordinator brief requirement:** Include this instruction explicitly: "If the band is too loud during dinner, you have full authority to ask them to reduce volume. This is standard and expected — a professional act will respond without issue."

---

### Speeches

**Function:** Music introduces each speaker and holds the room between speakers. One of the most emotionally significant parts of the day — music can elevate it significantly when handled well.

**The DJ owns speeches, not the band:** The DJ manages this entire period — speaker intro songs, fading in and out, background music between speakers, transition back to dinner or dancing. Requires an actively engaged DJ who is listening, watching, and anticipating. Not background operation.

**Speaker intro music:** Works well when properly briefed. Requires a complete list — each speaker matched to a specific song — provided in advance, not on the day.

**Between speakers:** If gaps are long, DJ plays background music consistent with the couple's taste profile. Not silence, not dance music.

**The 10-minute rule:** Coordinator alerts the band 10 minutes before they return to the stage so they are ready.

**Coordinator brief requirement:** Include speaker list with intro songs as a named deliverable. Flag explicitly: this period requires an actively engaged DJ. State: "Share the full speaker order and song list with your DJ at least one week before the wedding."

**Primary failure mode:** DJ not plugged into the moment — wrong timing on fades, missing cues. Entirely preventable with proper briefing.

---

### First Dance

**Function:** A cherished personal moment for the couple, witnessed by their guests. For some couples includes a choreographed routine. It honours the marriage in a visible, intimate way.

**Live vs PA rule:** If the couple has a specific pre-rehearsed song — especially if choreographed — PA only, always. Timing and arrangement matter too much to risk a live interpretation. This rule applies equally to all one-on-one dances — parent dances, bridal party dances.

**Song selection:** Something they both love and want to dance to, something that speaks to them. The practical test: have they danced to this song before? Does it feel right at the length it runs?

**The length problem — critical failure mode:** Songs that go on too long with the couple alone on the floor. The couple feels exposed, guests feel uncomfortable, the moment fizzles. DJ must be briefed to read the room and fade at the right moment — typically after one verse and chorus, or when the couple signals. This is a named responsibility in the coordinator brief.

**Transition in:** MC or best man announces. DJ has the song ready. Clean cue, immediate start. No gap.

**Additional dances:** DJ handles all one-on-one dances, fading one into the next unless coordinator specifies otherwise.

**The transition out — the most important 10 seconds of the night:** If a band is playing the dance set, they are on stage and ready. As the last first dance ends, they count off the first song and ignite the dance floor. The energy shift from intimate to celebratory must be immediate and deliberate. A fumbled transition here kills the momentum for the entire dancing portion of the night. Brief this precisely.

**Coordinator brief requirement:** Name the song. Name the cue person. Brief the DJ explicitly on the fade-out moment. Brief the band or DJ explicitly on the transition into dancing.

---

### Dancing

**Function:** Party, joy, celebration. A dance floor that catches fire has enough people dancing to sustain the vibe — peaks and valleys are fine, the floor never empties completely.

**Start with the music, not the format:** What music does the couple want? The music determines the format. If the music is electronic, deep house, or dance — DJ only. If it translates well live — Motown, funk, soul, pop, some hip hop — a band can be exceptional.

**Live band minimum for dancing:** Five piece minimum. Keys or guitar, bass, drums, vocals, horn. Anything under 5 piece cannot sustain the variety, intensity, and energy required for 70–90 minutes of dancing. This is a firm rule.

**Backing tracks:** If the band uses backing tracks, the music sounds closer to the original recording. Flag this to the couple as a question to ask — it affects the feel of the live experience significantly.

**The 70–90 minute rule:** One long set for the dance band, then DJ takeover. The DJ takeover is a strategic programming decision, not a downgrade. A skilled DJ reads the band's last song and mixes into something appropriate.

**DJ only:** Works exceptionally well for dancing. Maximum flexibility in genre and song selection. Requires an experienced wedding DJ who understands the job is to play to the whole room across generations and tastes — not to impose a pre-existing playlist, not to serve only the couple's specific taste at the expense of the floor. A good DJ reads what people respond to, knows when to lift energy and when to give the room a breather, accommodates reasonable requests, and keeps the floor populated.

**DJ PLUS:** Not recommended. See DJ PLUS section above.

**The avoidance list:** Essential. Must be collected from the couple and communicated directly to the DJ. Non-negotiable deliverable in every coordinator brief.

**Coordinator brief requirement:** Include the couple's music taste profile, the avoidance list, and this instruction: "Give your DJ the emotional and stylistic territory the couple wants to stay in — not just a song list. A great DJ uses the brief as a map, not a script. Trust them to manage the energy — volume and intensity on the dance floor is their call to make, not yours to manage."

---

### Last Song

**Function:** A send-off, a final peak, and a collective moment — the remaining guests coming together to share one last celebratory experience. The emotional full stop on the day.

**Who plays it:** DJ. The band's set ended 70–90 minutes ago. This is DJ territory.

**Song selection:** Something meaningful to the couple. Named in advance — never left to the DJ to choose.

**How it arrives — three valid approaches:**
Announced: DJ lets the room know the wedding is coming to an end, gives people a chance to gather on the floor.
Felt: guests know from the time and the natural wind-down — no announcement needed.
Earned: the DJ has been gradually shifting tempo and energy, and this song is the emotional peak of that arc.

The DJ reads which approach the room needs. Brief them on all three and give them permission to choose.

**Logistics:** Lights down, not up. All remaining guests to the floor. The music and the moment lead. Lights up and music out comes after the song ends, not during it.

**Coordinator brief requirement:** Name the song. Brief the DJ on the three approaches. State: "Read the room — let the moment arrive naturally rather than announcing it mechanically."

**Primary failure mode:** DJ plays a pre-determined song mechanically without reading whether the room is ready for it. Preventable — song is named in the brief, DJ is briefed on approach.

## The Song Question — Deep-Dive Addition

A song question must be added at the end of each moment deep-dive. Songs are the ground truth that turns theory into specific, actionable guidance. Without actual song choices, the MIL reasons in the abstract — it cannot evaluate whether a song translates live, whether the ensemble can do it justice, or what the Spotify playlist should contain.

Four songs per moment is the right number — enough to establish a pattern without overwhelming.

Songs serve three functions in the MIL:
1. They inform the live vs PA decision for that moment.
2. They determine the instrumentation — the song leads, the ensemble follows.
3. They populate the Spotify playlist for that moment.

**The question copy — brand-aligned, moment-specific:**

Songs are one of the highest-value inputs in the entire session. The copy must feel like a natural, warm continuation of the conversation — not a data collection field. Use the couple's language. Open with feeling, not logistics. Make the act of choosing songs feel meaningful, not administrative.

**Master framing principle:** "If you could hear four songs during [this moment], what would they be?" This is more evocative than "What songs do you want played?" — it surfaces genuine taste rather than what couples think they're supposed to say. It also doesn't presuppose live vs recorded, which matters for the MIL's evaluation.

**Moment-specific question copy:**

*Guest Arrivals:*
"As your guests arrive — before the day has really begun — what four songs would be playing in the background? The ones that would immediately say: something special is about to happen."

*Ceremony:*
"If you could choose four songs for the ceremony — the music playing as people take their seats, as you walk in, as you sign — which ones feel right? They don't have to be 'wedding songs'. They just have to be yours."

*Pre-Drinks:*
"Your guests are outside, drinks in hand, the day still unfolding. What four songs would you want drifting across the space? The ones that would set exactly the right mood while you're off having your photos taken."

*Your Entrance:*
"The doors open. You walk in as married. What song is playing? (And if you have a second choice waiting in the wings — tell us that too.)"
Note: this moment takes 1–2 songs, not four. Adjust the question accordingly.

*Dinner:*
"Picture your guests around the tables, the room full, everyone settling in. What four songs would be playing softly in the background — the ones that would make dinner feel exactly the way you want it to?"

*Speeches:*
"Some couples love giving each speaker their own entrance song — something personal that gets a laugh or sets the tone. If you wanted to do that, which songs come to mind? And if you'd rather keep it simple, tell us that too."
Note: this moment is about speaker intro songs specifically. The question acknowledges both approaches.

*First Dance:*
"Your first dance as a married couple. Which song is it? And if you haven't decided yet — what are you choosing between?"
Note: this is a single-song moment. The question surfaces the song or the shortlist.

*Dancing:*
"The floor is open, the night is young. What four songs, if the DJ played them back to back, would have everyone on the floor? These are the songs that define what kind of night you want."

*Last Song:*
"The last song of the night — the one the room carries home. What is it for you? No wrong answer. Just the one that feels right."
Note: this is a single-song moment.

**Copy rules for all song questions:**
- Never use the word "playlist" — it sounds administrative.
- Never use "perfect" or "ideal."
- "Yours" is powerful — use it.
- Short sentences. One idea per question.
- Acknowledge that they don't have to know yet — the MIL uses whatever they give it.
- If the field is left blank, the MIL notes this warmly and recommends based on the discovery session taste signals only.

## MIL Behaviour When Songs Are Provided

When song choices are provided for a moment, the MIL must:

1. Evaluate whether each song translates well to the recommended ensemble. If it doesn't, say so honestly and explain why.
2. Use the songs to confirm or adjust the format recommendation — live vs PA, ensemble configuration.
3. Reference the songs specifically in the recommendation copy. Never generic. "Your choice of [song] for the pre-drinks tells us you want something with warmth and movement — that points toward a jazz duo rather than a solo pianist."
4. Flag any tension between songs — if four songs point in different directions, acknowledge it. "Two of your choices lean acoustic and intimate, two lean big and anthemic. We'd recommend talking to your act about how they handle that range."
5. Pass songs to Spotify playlist generation for that moment.

When song choices are not provided, the MIL uses the discovery session taste signals — home_listening, guilty_pleasure, crowd_vs_taste, musical_confidence — and notes that adding songs would sharpen the recommendation.

## What Changes in MIL System Prompt Behaviour

These are the specific behavioural changes this framework introduces. Each is a discrete rule addition to the MIL system prompt.

1. DJ PLUS removed as a standard recommendation. Bold direction only, rare circumstances.
2. Live vs PA golden rule: evaluate every song against the available ensemble before recommending live.
3. Function vs feature rule: unamplified or roaming acts are features with defined durations, never primary room carriers.
4. Solo musician ceiling: never recommend solo as primary carrier for dinner with 80+ guests or dancing.
5. Instrumentation logic: recommend ensemble configurations (2a, 2b, 2c) not just headcount.
6. Arc principle: offer PA-for-arrivals / live-for-ceremony as a considered creative choice, not a budget option.
7. Song-led recommendations: when songs are provided, the song determines the format and instrumentation.
8. Five vetting questions: surface for every live act recommendation.
9. Coordinator brief specificity: every moment brief must name the cue person, the song, and the transition instruction.
10. Volume instruction: dinner briefs only must explicitly empower the coordinator to manage band volume. Dancing briefs must do the opposite — instruct the coordinator to trust the DJ or band's energy and volume decisions on the floor.
